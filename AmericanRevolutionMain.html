New Jersey and the Revolutionary War (Link, Secondary)

- After the stamp act, the group of the Sons of Liberty formed to combat that of the British, and they used many New Jerseyan spots to meet and talk strategy.
- Alexander Hamiltion met Aaron Burr in Old Academy in Elizabeth in the summer of 1773.

The Greenwich Tea Burning

  • At this point, people were boycotting the British imports of tea (This is after the Boston Tea party on December 16, 1773), and the First Continental Congress had found out that a British Sympathizer by the name of Daniel Bowen was hiding tea for the British.
  • The continental congress, now presumably the governing factor of the colonies, was determining what to do with this information, but were interrupted as members of the congress decided to take matters into their own hands, burning the tea that Bowen hid.
  • The individuals responsible were taken for criminal charges, but later absolved due to the public state.

The Battle for Independence

  • British and Hessian Forces cross the Hudson River to invade New Jersey; American Forces retreat from fort lee and take residence in Newark
  • On December 23, 1776, the Battle of Iron Works Hill (Secondary Source, Author William M Dwyer) ensued. While the British and Hessian (German soldier for hire) forces eventually drove out the American force of 600, this skirmish allowed Washington to conduct his battle of Trenton on December 25/26, 1776.

The “Ten Crucial Days”: December 25, 1776 - January 3, 1777

The battles of this period of time changed the direction of the war and acted to lift the spirits of those in the Americas, much of whom felt like British victory was inevitable. (See here for letter between New Jerseyans before the crucial days (Primary))
These crucial battles are:
  • The Battle of Trenton
  • The Battle of Assunpink Creek (Second Battle of Trenton)
  • The Battle of Princeton

After winning these wars, it boosted the sentiments of the public and the militia, invigorating patriotic spirit (Washington Letter to the Public, Primary), and caused a rallying effect for remaining troops and new troops to continue fighting whilst allowing backing from the French to be secured by that of American diplomats.

After the patriots had successfully acquired foreign aid through the help of international diplomats and foreign entanglements, they were able to win the battle of Monmouth with the aid of the French (mostly voluntary officers and individuals such as Lafayette) on the 28 of June, 1778. (Record of a Revolutionary War Soldier:, Primary), (French Participation in the Battle of Monmouth, Secondary)

In all, during the American Revolution, New Jersey played a crucial role in the Revolutionary War as it served as a meeting place for important figures, a further reason to enter the war, and the site which influenced the patriots to persist and ultimately allowed them to win the war.
